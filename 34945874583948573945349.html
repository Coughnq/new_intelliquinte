Pollution Started

PFAS (Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are synthetic "forever chemicals" used in industrial applications since the 1940s. They never break down naturally in the environment or human body.

Environmental Impacts

Birds

  • Brown pelicans: Reduced hatching success
  • Tree swallows: Reproductive failures
  • Peregrine falcons: Thyroid disruption
  • Seabirds: Decreased body mass

Reptiles & Aquatic Life

  • American alligators: Autoimmune problems
  • Fish consumption advisories issued
  • Dangerous PFOS levels in freshwater fish
  • Bioaccumulation throughout food chain

Latest Discovery (2023)

University of North Carolina researchers identified eight completely new types of PFAS in Cape Fear River that the EPA had never documented before — suggesting contamination is far worse than originally understood.

Health Effects Linked to PFAS Exposure

  • Immune System: Weakened immune responses, reduced vaccine effectiveness
  • Cardiovascular: Elevated cholesterol, increased heart disease risk
  • Reproductive: Decreased fetal growth, pregnancy complications
  • Cancer: Kidney and testicular cancer associations
  • Liver: Liver damage and elevated enzyme levels

Forever Impact

PFAS persist in the human body for years to decades. Current contamination will affect multiple generations of North Carolinians, creating a public health legacy that extends far beyond the initial exposure period.

Solutions: The 3 R's Approach

REDUCE
  • Choose PFAS-free cookware (cast iron, stainless steel)
  • Avoid stain-resistant fabrics and carpets
  • Support PFAS restriction legislation
  • Purchase from environmentally responsible companies
REUSE
  • Install water-saving fixtures and systems
  • Collect rainwater for non-potable uses
  • Support closed-loop manufacturing processes
  • Implement community water conservation programs
RECYCLE
  • Proper disposal at hazardous waste collection centers
  • Support development of PFAS destruction technology
  • Advocate for circular economy principles
  • Participate in community collection programs

Policy Solution

Federal legislation to phase out PFAS production — similar to the Montreal Protocol's successful elimination of ozone-depleting chemicals — could prevent future contamination while cleanup and remediation efforts continue.
